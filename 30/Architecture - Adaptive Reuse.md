# 🏚️ Architecture — Project: Adaptive Reuse & Renovation
### *Design Within What Already Exists*

---

> **Core Idea:**  Too often developers will bulldoze and erase what was there, along with all of its history to make what for a cookie-cutter house that they build 100s of.   Sometimes it is important to keep the history of the building and add on to it, make a addition or perform an interior remodel because a new building in a 1990s neighborhood will not blend in and appear like it has always been there.

## 📋 Project Overview

In this project, you're not starting from scratch — you're starting from something that already exists. You'll choose a real building type, give it a new purpose that serves a genuine community need, and redesign it from the inside out. The challenge is learning to design within constraints you didn't create. That's exactly what architects do every day.

## Phase 1 — Building Type Selection and Background Research

### What You're Doing
You're choosing the type of building you want to reimagine. You don't need to find a specific building to measure, but you should choose a type that has recognizable and consistent physical characteristics — something you can research and understand before you start designing.

### Choosing Your Building Type
Good choices for this project have a few things in common: they're common enough that you can find examples and research their typical characteristics, and they're interesting enough that you can imagine them becoming something completely different. Consider:

- **Strip mall unit / small commercial block** — wide open floor plates, parking-focused sites
- **Warehouse or light industrial** — high ceilings, large structural bays, loading access
- **Single-storey school or community hall** — large open rooms, institutional character
- **Motel** — repetitive unit structure, exterior corridor access, abundant parking
- **Church or place of worship** — dramatic interior volumes, often in residential areas
- **Fire hall** — large apparatus bays, mix of residential and operational spaces

### Background Research
Once you've chosen your type, research its typical physical characteristics. You need to understand what you're working with before you can redesign it. Investigate:
- Standard structural bay widths and column spacing
- Typical ceiling heights *(this is huge — high ceilings open up many possibilities)*
- What the exterior is usually made of *(masonry, metal cladding, tilt-up concrete?)*
- How the building typically sits on its site *(setbacks, parking, loading)*
- The construction era and any character features worth preserving

### Precedent Research
Find two or three real-world examples of your building type being successfully adapted for a new use. These don't have to be in Canada — there are amazing adaptive reuse projects all over the world. For each precedent, note:
- What the building was originally
- What it became
- What physical features were kept
- What was changed
- Include images where possible

> 💡 **Tip:** Search terms like *"warehouse to loft conversion"*, *"church to residential"*, or *"school adaptive reuse"* will return great examples. [ArchDaily.com](https://www.archdaily.com) is an excellent resource for high-quality architectural projects with photos and drawings.

- [ ] Building type profile *(physical characteristics, structural system, typical dimensions)*
- [ ] Precedent research — two to three examples with images and notes

## Phase 2 — New Use Investigation and Community Need

### What You're Doing
The most important question in adaptive reuse is *why*. You need to identify a genuine community or human need that your reimagined building will serve. This isn't about picking something that sounds cool — it's about understanding what people actually need and how your building can provide it.

### Finding a Real Need
Think about your community. What is missing, underserved, or desperately needed? Some ideas that have worked well in real adaptive reuse projects:

- **Affordable housing** — converting a motel or office building into apartment units
- **Artist live-work studios** — warehouse to mixed residential and studio space
- **Neighbourhood food hub** — strip mall to market hall with food vendors and community kitchen
- **Youth drop-in centre** — former school to youth programming and recreation space
- **Coworking and maker space** — industrial building to shared workspace with tools and studios
- **Childcare facility** — commercial building to licensed daycare with outdoor play area
- **Transitional or supportive housing** — motel to small-unit housing with support services

### The Fit Test
Your new use needs to make sense for the building type you've chosen. Ask yourself:
- Does the building's size fit the scale of this use?
- Do the structural characteristics *(ceiling heights, bay widths, floor loads)* support what you're imagining?
- Is the site location appropriate — would this use want to be in an industrial area, a commercial strip, a residential neighbourhood?

### Client and User Profile
Define who will use the building after it's been reimagined. Be specific. If it's housing, who lives there? If it's a community space, who runs it and who comes? Understanding your users drives your design decisions the same way a client profile does in a new-build project.

### Initial Code Considerations
When a building changes its use, the building code treats it differently. Start thinking about what code requirements apply to your new use. Key areas to note at this stage:
- Occupancy classification change *(e.g., from industrial to residential — very different requirements)*
- Accessibility requirements for the new use
- Fire separation and egress requirements
- Plumbing and washroom requirements for the new occupancy

## Phase 3 — Existing Conditions Plan and Constraint Mapping

### What You're Doing
Before you design anything new, you need to fully understand what you're working with. This phase is about documenting the existing building as accurately as possible, then analyzing it to find both the constraints and the opportunities hidden within it.

### Existing Conditions Floor Plan
Draw a scaled floor plan of the building as it currently exists. You're not drawing your new design here — you're documenting the original. Include:
- All structural walls and columns *(these likely can't move)*
- Existing door and window openings
- Any fixed elements — stairs, mechanical rooms, loading docks, washrooms
- Overall dimensions and ceiling heights noted
- North arrow

### Constraint and Opportunity Map
This is the most important drawing in this phase. Take your existing conditions plan and annotate it to identify three categories:

| Must Stay | Could Change | Opportunities |
|---|---|---|
| Structural walls | Non-structural partitions | High ceilings for mezzanines |
| Load-bearing columns | Window locations and sizes | North skylights for studios |
| Floor slab | Entry points | Large bays for open plans |
| Foundation | Interior finishes | Loading dock as covered entry |
| Exterior envelope *(usually)* | Mechanical and plumbing | Exposed structure as character |

> 💡 **Tip:** The best adaptive reuse projects don't fight their constraints — they celebrate them. A column grid that can't move becomes a rhythm that organizes the new floor plan. An existing clerestory window becomes the feature around which new common areas gather. Start asking: *what does this building want to become?*

### ✅ Phase 3 Deliverables
- [ ] Existing conditions floor plan *(scaled, with all structural and fixed elements)*
- [ ] Annotated constraint and opportunity map
- [ ] Written analysis of key constraints and the opportunities they create

## Phase 4 — Design Brief

### What You're Doing
With your building understood and your new use established, you're ready to write a formal design brief. This document brings everything together and becomes the reference point for every design decision that follows.

### What Your Design Brief Should Include

#### Project Summary
One paragraph describing what this project is: what the building was, what it will become, and why this transformation matters.

#### Community Need
A clear statement of the human or community need your design will serve, supported by evidence or reasoning about why this need exists.

#### User / Client Description
Who will use this building? Who is the client *(could be a non-profit, a developer, a community organization)*? What are their priorities?

#### Constraints Summary
Key physical constraints inherited from the existing building that your design must work within.

#### Code and Zoning Notes
The main code considerations identified in Phase 2, and any zoning issues relevant to the change of use.

#### Design Intentions
Two or three clear statements about what this project is trying to achieve beyond just fitting the program in. What will make this design successful?

#### Building Program
A room-by-room list of all spaces required for the new use, with approximate sizes and key functional relationships. Check this against your existing floor plate to confirm it fits.

### ✅ Phase 4 Deliverables
- [ ] Complete written design brief
- [ ] Building program *(room list with approximate areas and relationships)*
- [ ] Feasibility check — does the program fit the existing building footprint?

## Phase 5 — Concept Development

### What You're Doing
Now you start generating ideas for how the new use will inhabit the existing building. Same rule as any design phase: **volume before refinement.** Your thumbnails should explore different ways of organizing the program within the existing footprint — not just one approach.

### What Your Thumbnails Should Explore
- Different circulation strategies — where do people enter, how do they move through the building?
- Different ways of handling the relationship between old and new — what gets exposed and celebrated versus covered or removed?
- How outdoor space is activated — courtyard, rooftop, forecourt, loading dock transformed into covered entry?
- How the exterior changes to signal the new identity while respecting the existing shell
- Where the new use requires the most significant intervention versus where it can work with what's already there

### Key Questions to Ask Yourself
- What do you want to preserve or celebrate from the original building's character?
- Where does your new use require you to intervene most significantly?
- What is the spatial experience of arriving at and entering the reimagined building?
- How does the building signal its new purpose from the street?

> 💡 **Tip:** Annotate your thumbnails with notes about what each approach preserves, what it changes, and why. For adaptive reuse, the reasoning behind your choices is as important as the choices themselves. Show your thinking on the page.

### Selecting and Justifying Your Direction
After producing at least 4–6 thumbnails, select one direction and write a rationale explaining how this concept responds to the existing building and the needs of the new users. What does it preserve? What does it change? Why?

### ✅ Phase 5 Deliverables
- [ ] Minimum 4–6 annotated thumbnail concept sketches
- [ ] Written concept selection rationale

## Phase 6 — Design Development

### What You're Doing
With your concept selected, you now develop it into a complete set of architectural drawings. These need to be accurate, to scale, properly labelled, and dimensioned. In an adaptive reuse project, your drawings need to clearly communicate what is existing, what is being removed, and what is new — this distinction is central to the project.

### New Floor Plan
**Scale: 1:50 or 1:100**

Draw your new floor plan over or alongside the existing conditions plan, clearly distinguishing new construction from existing elements. Use different line weights *(heavy for new walls, lighter for existing)* or different colours. All rooms must be labeled and dimensioned. Verify that your building program is fully accounted for. Accessibility and egress requirements should be visible in the layout — check that every occupied space has a path to exit the building.

### Elevations *(minimum 2 — front and one other)*
**Scale: same as floor plan**

Show how the exterior of the building has been transformed. These are the drawings that communicate the relationship between old and new most powerfully. Show what was kept and what is new — new glazing cut into an existing masonry wall, a new entrance canopy, a rooftop addition set back from the parapet. Material callouts are required. These decisions need to be explained, not just drawn.

### Building Section
**Scale: 1:50 or 1:100**

Cut through the building to show interior ceiling heights, floor levels, and how the structural system of the existing building has been worked with or worked around. In adaptive reuse, the section is often the most revealing drawing because it shows how the new life fits inside the old bones. If your design adds a mezzanine to take advantage of high ceilings, or a rooftop addition, this is where it shows best.

### Demolition / Change Plan
**Scale: same as floor plan**

This drawing shows what is being removed, what is being retained, and what is new — typically using three line types or colours. This is standard professional practice in renovation projects and adds a layer of authenticity to your work. It also makes your design decisions explicit: you have to commit to exactly what stays and what goes.

> 💡 **Tip:** Use a simple legend on your demolition plan — for example, **grey = existing retained**, **red dashed = demolished**, **black = new construction**. This makes the drawing immediately readable to anyone who picks it up.

## Phase 7 — Presentation, 3D Model & Portfolio

### 3D Printed Model — Part A: Floor Plan with Two-Colour Walls

Your physical model for this project tells a **before and after story** — and that's what makes it especially powerful. You'll print two sets of walls in two different colours to make the design intervention immediately visible to anyone who looks at the model.

#### Preparing the Floor Plan Base
Produce a clean digital version of your new floor plan at 1:50 or 1:100 scale. The base should distinguish between existing elements and new construction — use different shading or line colours, and include a printed legend. Print on cardstock and mount on foam core or MDF.

#### The Two Sets of Walls

| Existing Walls *(Retained)* | New Walls *(Added)* |
|---|---|
| Print in grey or concrete tone | Print in white or warm tone |
| Represents the original structure | Represents your design intervention |
| Heavier, more solid feel | Shows where you've added or changed |
| Includes all load-bearing elements | Includes new partitions and enclosures |

#### Modeling Your Walls
Model walls as rectangular boxes with openings subtracted for doors and windows. At 1:100 scale, a standard 2.7m wall prints **27mm tall**. If a wall in your design has a new opening cut through an existing wall, show that opening in the model — this communicates the renovation logic better than any drawing can. If a new glazed facade replaces a blank wall, the contrast between the solid existing wall and the open new framing should be visible in the model.

> 💡 **Tip:** Model both sets of walls in the same software file using the same reference point — this ensures the two sets line up perfectly when placed on the floor plan base.

---

### 3D Printed Model — Part B: Scaled Site Model

The site model for adaptive reuse focuses on the building's relationship to its urban or suburban context. Print your site plan at 1:200 or 1:500 and mount it on a rigid base. Show any site changes — new entry sequence, parking reconfiguration, added landscaping, outdoor activation spaces — on the printed base.

Print a simplified massing model of the building as it appears after renovation. If your design includes a rooftop addition, exterior canopy, or significant facade change, these should be visible. Consider printing simplified versions of immediately adjacent buildings at the same scale to show your building in its neighbourhood context — this small addition makes the site model significantly more compelling.

---

### 📸 Before and After Documentation

Because this is a renovation project, document your model assembly as a **three-stage photograph series**:

| Stage | What to Photograph |
|---|---|
| **Stage 1 — Before** | The printed floor plan base before any walls are placed |
| **Stage 2 — Existing** | After the existing *(grey)* walls are placed |
| **Stage 3 — After** | After the new *(white)* walls are added |

This series mirrors how a real renovation unfolds in sequence and makes a powerful portfolio element that shows the transformation at a glance.

> 💡 **Tip:** The two-colour wall model is your best presentation tool. When you place it in front of your critique audience, they can immediately see where you intervened and where you deferred to the existing structure. That visual distinction drives the most interesting critique conversations — be ready to explain the reasoning behind every choice.

### Final Presentation
Your critique presentation tells the story of a building's transformation: what it was, what need it now serves, what the constraints were, and how your design worked with them. The before-and-after dimension of this project makes for a compelling narrative — use it. Be ready to explain not just what you designed but why you kept what you kept and changed what you changed.

## 🖨️ Student Guide: Preparing Files for 3D Printing

> The key difference in this project vs. a new-build project is that you're printing **two separate sets of walls in two separate colours**. Read this before you start modeling.

### Step 1 — Confirm Your Scale
Before anything else, confirm the scale you're working at. **1:100 is recommended for the floor plan model.**

| Real Life | 1:100 Model |
|---|---|
| 1 metre | 10 mm |
| 2.7 m wall height | 27 mm |
| 6 m structural bay | 60 mm |
| 200 mm wall thickness | 2 mm *(print at 3–4 mm for strength)* |

### Step 2 — Prepare Your Floor Plan Base
Finalize your new floor plan in your drawing software, showing both existing and new elements in different colours or line weights. Export as PDF and print on cardstock at your chosen scale. Your printed base should include a legend. Mount on 5mm foam core or 3mm MDF with spray adhesive or PVA glue.

### Step 3 — Model Your Existing Walls
Open **Tinkercad** *(free, browser-based)*, **SketchUp**, or your preferred 3D software. Model all the existing walls being retained in your design as rectangular boxes with openings subtracted for existing doors and windows. Keep wall thickness at **3–4 mm minimum** for printability. Export as `existing_walls.stl`. This set will print in your first colour *(grey or concrete tone recommended)*.

### Step 4 — Model Your New Walls
In the same software file, model all the walls and enclosures that represent your new design intervention. This includes new partitions, infill between existing structural elements, and any new exterior additions. If you've cut a new opening through an existing wall, model the remaining wall segments with the opening visible. Export as `new_walls.stl`. This set will print in your second colour *(white or warm tone recommended)*.

### Step 5 — Export and Slice
Open each STL in your slicer software *(Cura, PrusaSlicer, Bambu Studio)*. Check that dimensions are correct — walls should be 27mm tall at 1:100. Recommended settings:

| Setting | Value |
|---|---|
| Layer height | 0.15–0.20 mm |
| Infill | 15–20% |
| Supports | Only if needed |
| Brim | Recommended for thin walls |

Slice each set separately so you can change filament colour between prints.

### Step 6 — Print, Change Filament, Print Again
Print your existing walls first in your chosen *"old building"* colour. When complete, change the filament to your *"new construction"* colour and print the new walls. If your printer doesn't support mid-print colour changes, simply print each set as a separate job.

### Step 7 — Dry Fit, Then Assemble
Before gluing anything, place all walls on the floor plan base for a dry fit. Check that openings align with drawn doors and windows, and that the two sets of walls fit together correctly at their junctions. Trim any elephant foot with a craft knife. Glue existing walls first, let set, then add new walls. **Photograph all three stages.**

### Step 8 — Site Massing Model
For the site model, create a simplified exterior massing block of your renovated building at 1:200 or 1:500. Print in a neutral colour. Mount your site plan on cardstock and foam core. If your design has an addition or rooftop element, make it visible in the massing. Consider printing simple box forms for adjacent buildings to show urban context.

---

### 🔧 Common Problems and Fixes

| Problem | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Two wall sets don't fit together at junctions | Modeled in separate files with misaligned origins | Model both sets in the same file using the same reference point |
| Walls too fragile and break on removal from bed | Wall thickness below 2–3 mm | Increase wall thickness; use brim for bed adhesion |
| Openings don't align with floor plan print | Scale error between model and base print | Re-verify scale; reprint base before gluing walls |
| Colours look too similar | Filament choices not contrasting enough | Use high contrast: dark grey vs. white, or natural vs. bright white |
| Model looks messy at wall junctions | Walls not trimmed cleanly to meet | Use craft knife and sandpaper to clean junction points before assembly |
| Print warps or lifts off bed | Bed adhesion issue | Use brim setting; ensure bed is level and clean |

> 💡 **Tip:** Photograph your model at all three assembly stages — base only, existing walls placed, and new walls added. These photos belong in your portfolio and are some of the most compelling images in any adaptive reuse presentation. They show the transformation in three frames, which is exactly what this project is about.
