# 🏡 Architecture — Project: Site-Responsive Design
### *Let the Land Lead*

---

> **Core Idea:** Far too often, we see building bulldozing the land flat like SimCity instead working with the land.  It is important to see what the land offers, it will tell you how to water flows and how the land moves so that you do not have flooding or landslides in the future.
---

## 📋 Project Overview

In this project, the land tells you how to build. You will choose a real site, study everything about it — the sun, wind, slope, views, and neighbours — and then design a building that belongs there. Every major decision in your design should be traceable back to what the site taught you.  A well thought out design can often make the building blend into the landscape and make it appear like it was always meant to be there.

## Phase 1 — Site Selection and Initial Investigation

### What You're Doing
You're choosing your site. This is one of the most important decisions in the whole project, so take it seriously. Think about what kind of site genuinely interests you — a steep hillside, a narrow city lot squeezed between buildings, a lakeside acreage, or a corner lot in an established neighbourhood. Your site can be a real place or a plausible one, but it should feel grounded and specific.

Once you've chosen your site, you'll create a **site inventory** — a factual record of everything that currently exists there. Think of it as a fact-finding mission before you start making any design decisions.

### Your Site Inventory Should Include
- Lot dimensions and shape
- Topography (flat, sloped, terraced?)
- Existing vegetation — trees, shrubs, grass
- Neighbouring structures and their heights
- Street access and direction
- Any water features, drainage ditches, or natural elements

> 💡 **Tip:** Google Maps satellite view and Google Earth are great tools for studying a real site. Many municipalities also have free GIS (mapping) tools online where you can find lot dimensions, zoning, and topography data.

### ✅ Phase 1 Deliverables
- [ ] Written site selection rationale *(why did you choose this site?)*
- [ ] Base site plan *(rough sketch or printed map is fine at this stage)*
- [ ] Site inventory notes

## Phase 2 — Site Analysis

### What You're Doing
Phase 1 told you *what is there*. Phase 2 answers the question: *what does it mean?* Site analysis takes all your inventory facts and interprets them through a design lens. This is where you start to understand what your site wants the building to be.

### Six Things to Investigate

#### ☀️ Sun and Shadow
Where does the sun rise and set relative to your lot? In our climate, south-facing windows capture the most winter sun. Where will shadows fall from neighbouring buildings or trees? This affects where you place living spaces, windows, and outdoor areas.

#### 💨 Wind
What direction do prevailing winds come from in this region? Are there seasonal differences? Wind affects where you place the main entry, how you design for natural ventilation in summer, and where outdoor spaces will actually be comfortable.

#### 🌊 Topography and Drainage
How does water move across and off your site? Where are the high and low points? This shapes where the building sits, how the foundation is handled, and whether you need retaining walls or grading.

#### 👁️ Views and Privacy
What does your client want to look at? What do they want to be screened from? A great view to the north might be worth prioritizing even if it sacrifices some solar gain. Neighbours, busy streets, and parking lots are things you'd want to screen.

#### 🚗 Access and Circulation
Where do people arrive from? How do vehicles and pedestrians move through the site? Where does parking go without dominating the design? How do you create a welcoming entry sequence?

#### 🏘️ Context and Character
What does the surrounding neighbourhood look, feel, and function like? Is it dense urban, quiet suburban, or rural? What are the existing building heights and setbacks? Your design should be aware of its context even if it doesn't copy it.

> 💡 **Tip:** Your main deliverable for this phase is an **annotated site analysis drawing** — take your base site plan and layer on arrows, notes, symbols, and diagrams showing sun paths, wind direction, views, noise sources, and more. Architects call this a "site analysis diagram" and it's one of the first things a real firm produces on any project.

### ✅ Phase 2 Deliverables
- [ ] Annotated site analysis drawing *(base plan with layered notes and diagrams)*
- [ ] Written summary of key findings and what they mean for your design

## Phase 3 — Design Brief and Client Profile

### What You're Doing
Now that you understand your site, you need to decide who is building there and why. Creating a specific, believable client is one of the most important things you can do to make your design meaningful. A vague client leads to a vague design. A specific client gives every decision a reason.

### Creating Your Client Profile
Your client profile should be detailed enough to drive real design choices. Consider:
- Who are they? *(age, family structure, occupation, lifestyle)*
- How do they use their home? *(do they work from home, host often, have hobbies that need space?)*
- Do they have any accessibility needs?
- What is their cultural background and how might that influence spatial preferences?
- What is their aesthetic — minimalist, traditional, eclectic, industrial?
- What is their relationship to the outdoors?

### Writing Your Design Brief
The design brief is a professional document that captures everything your client needs. It becomes the measuring stick for every design decision you make. Your brief should include:
- Client needs, lifestyle, and priorities
- Accessibility requirements
- Aesthetic preferences and any styles they've referenced
- Special programmatic needs *(home office, workshop, studio, accessible bathroom, etc.)*
- Budget considerations *(even a general range)*
- Any site-specific preferences they've expressed

### Building Program
The building program is a room-by-room list of every space your design needs to include. For each space, note the approximate size in square metres and any key relationships *(e.g., "mudroom should connect directly to garage and kitchen")*. This becomes your checklist when you evaluate your floor plan later.

### ✅ Phase 3 Deliverables
- [ ] Written client profile
- [ ] Written design brief covering all requirements
- [ ] Building program *(room list with approximate sizes and relationships)*

## Phase 4 — Concept Development

### What You're Doing
This is where you start designing — but not by going straight to the final answer. The rule in Phase 4 is: **volume before refinement.** You need to generate lots of ideas quickly before you commit to one direction. The goal is to explore, not to perfect.

### How to Do Thumbnail Sketches
Thumbnail sketches are small, fast, rough drawings — they should take minutes, not hours. Each one should show:
- Where the building sits on the lot *(its footprint)*
- The general shape and massing
- Which direction the main windows face
- How the building relates to the street and the neighbours
- Any interesting moves — does it step with the slope? Does it have a courtyard? Two separate volumes?

### Questions to Push Your Thinking
- Where does the building sit on the lot, and why there specifically?
- How does it address the street — open and welcoming, or private and set back?
- Where are the main glazed faces and why? *(Remember your sun analysis!)*
- Does the building step with or push against the topography?
- Is it one form or multiple connected volumes?
- Where is the transition between public and private space?

> 💡 **Tip:** Annotate your thumbnails! A sketch without words is just a picture. Write notes directly on your sketches explaining the thinking behind each move. *"South-facing glazing for passive solar"* or *"garage pushed to north to shield from wind"* — these notes show that your design is responding to your site analysis, not just looking cool.

### Selecting Your Direction
After producing at least 4–6 thumbnails, choose one direction to develop. Write a short rationale *(a paragraph or two)* explaining why this concept best responds to your site analysis and your client's brief. What does this concept do that the others didn't?

### ✅ Phase 4 Deliverables
- [ ] Minimum 4–6 thumbnail concept sketches with annotations
- [ ] Written concept selection rationale

## Phase 5 — Design Development

### What You're Doing
With your concept chosen, you now develop it into a complete set of architectural drawings. These are your professional deliverables — they need to be accurate, to scale, properly labelled, and dimensioned. Think of these drawings as the set a contractor would use to actually build your design.

### Floor Plan
**Scale: 1:50 or 1:100**

Your floor plan is the most important drawing. It shows the layout of all rooms as seen from above, as if the roof has been removed and you're looking straight down. Include all walls, doors *(shown swinging open)*, windows, stairs, and major built-ins like kitchen counters and bathroom fixtures. Every room needs a label and overall dimensions are required. Your layout should reflect your site analysis — living areas oriented toward the sun, bedrooms away from street noise, etc.

### Elevations *(minimum 2 — front + one other)*
**Scale: same as floor plan**

Elevations show the exterior faces of your building as flat, straight-on views. They reveal the roofline, window proportions, material choices, and how the building meets the ground. Your elevations should show material callouts *(what is that cladding? what is the roof material?)* and any outdoor elements like decks, overhangs, or screen walls.

### Building Section
**Scale: 1:50 or 1:100**

A section is a cut-through view of the building — imagine slicing it in half with a giant knife and looking at the cut face. It shows interior ceiling heights, floor-to-floor relationships, roof structure, insulation, and how the building responds to any grade changes. Choose your cut line to reveal the most interesting or complex part of your design.

### Site Plan
**Scale: 1:200 or 1:500**

The site plan shows your building footprint within the full lot, including driveway, walkways, landscaping, outdoor living areas, parking, and any significant natural features. Show north arrow and scale bar. This is the drawing that confirms your building actually fits on the site and relates properly to the street and property lines.

## Phase 6 — Presentation, 3D Model & Portfolio

### 3D Printed Model — Part A: Floor Plan with Printed Walls

Your first model brings your floor plan to life as a physical, three-dimensional object. It consists of a **printed floor plan base** with **3D printed walls** placed on top of it.

#### Step 1 — Prepare Your Digital Floor Plan
Produce a clean digital version of your floor plan in whatever software you're using — AutoCAD, SketchUp, Tinkercad, or a scanned and cleaned-up hand drawing. Print at 1:50 or 1:100 scale on cardstock, then mount on a rigid base *(foam core or MDF)*.

#### Step 2 — Model and Print Your Walls
Using your floor plan as reference, model your walls as 3D printable objects. At 1:100 scale, a standard 2.7m wall prints **27mm tall** — keep wall height proportional to your chosen scale. Model door and window openings accurately so the model reflects your actual design. You don't need to print every interior partition, but include all exterior walls and any major interior walls that define the main spaces.

> 💡 **Tip:** Consider using two different filament colours to distinguish exterior walls from interior partitions — for example, white for exterior and grey for interior. This makes the model much easier to read at a glance.

---

### 3D Printed Model — Part B: Scaled Site Model

Your second model shows the building in its landscape context. Print your site plan at 1:200 or 1:500 and mount it on a rigid base. Then 3D print a simplified massing model of your building — showing the overall form and roofline — and place it on the site.

The site model should also show:
- Topographic changes *(use layered foam core contours cut from your site analysis)*
- Trees and vegetation *(simple printed forms or handmade)*
- Driveway, walkways, and outdoor living areas *(marked on the printed base)*
- North arrow

> 💡 **Tip:** Present both models together as a set. The detailed floor plan model shows interior organization; the site model shows how the building sits in its landscape. Together they tell the complete story of your design.

---

### Final Presentation
Your critique presentation should walk your audience through the full story of your project: the site, the client, the concept, and the design. Use your models as the centrepiece. Be ready to explain how your site analysis directly influenced your design decisions.

### Portfolio Compilation
Your portfolio is the complete record of your design process from start to finish. It should include work from every phase — not just the final drawings. A strong portfolio shows your thinking, not just your outcome. Include:
- Photographs of both models from multiple angles
- In-progress photos of printing and assembly
- All phase work from site inventory through final drawings
- Written reflection on site, environmental stewardship, and what you'd change

## 🖨️ Student Guide: Preparing Files for 3D Printing

> Read this before you start modeling your walls.

### Step 1 — Choose and Confirm Your Scale
Before you model anything, confirm the scale you're working at. **1:100 is the most common for this project.**

| Real Life | 1:100 Model |
|---|---|
| 1 metre | 10 mm |
| 2.7 m wall height | 27 mm |
| 5 m room width | 50 mm |
| 200 mm wall thickness | 2 mm *(print at 3–4 mm for strength)* |

### Step 2 — Prepare Your Floor Plan Base
Set your document to your chosen scale in CAD or drawing software. Export as PDF, then print on cardstock *(110–160 gsm is ideal)*. Mount your printed floor plan on a rigid base: 5mm foam core, 3mm MDF, or thin plywood. Use spray adhesive or PVA glue for a clean bond.

### Step 3 — Model Your Walls in 3D Software
Open **Tinkercad** *(free, browser-based — easiest for beginners)*, **SketchUp** *(more powerful)*, or any 3D modeling software you're comfortable with. Model each wall segment as a rectangular box. Key dimensions:
- **Length** — matching your floor plan
- **Thickness** — minimum 3–4 mm for printability
- **Height** — 27 mm at 1:100 for a 2.7 m ceiling

Model window and door openings as cutouts. In Tinkercad, use a **"hole"** shape to subtract the opening from the wall block.

### Step 4 — Export as STL
When your walls are modeled, export as an STL file.
- **Tinkercad:** Click Export → `.STL`
- **SketchUp:** Use the STL Exporter plugin *(free from Extension Warehouse)*

Name files clearly: `exterior_walls.stl`, `interior_walls.stl`, etc.

### Step 5 — Slice Your File
Open your STL in your slicer *(Cura, PrusaSlicer, Bambu Studio)*. Check that scale looks correct. Recommended settings:

| Setting | Value |
|---|---|
| Layer height | 0.15–0.20 mm |
| Infill | 15–20% |
| Supports | Only if needed *(most wall models won't)* |
| Brim | Recommended for thin walls |

### Step 6 — Print and Assemble
Once printed, do a **dry fit** on your floor plan base before gluing anything. Check that openings align with drawn doors and windows. Trim any elephant foot with a craft knife if needed. Glue walls in place using PVA or super glue. Let dry fully before photographing.

### Step 7 — Site Model
For the site massing model, create a simplified block of your building's exterior form at your site plan scale *(1:200 or 1:500)*. Print in a neutral colour. Represent topography with layered foam core strips. Place massing model on site and secure.

### 🔧 Common Problems and Fixes

| Problem | Likely Cause | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| Walls too thin and snap | Wall thickness below 2 mm | Scale up wall thickness to 3–4 mm minimum |
| Model is wrong size when sliced | Units mismatch between software and slicer | Check that slicer is set to mm, not inches |
| Openings don't line up with floor plan | Scale error in model or base print | Re-check scale calculation; reprint base if needed |
| Print warps or lifts off bed | Bed adhesion issue | Use brim setting; ensure bed is level and clean |
| Walls won't stay upright on base | Bottom surface not flat or insufficient glue | Sand base of walls flat; clamp while drying |

> 💡 **Tip:** Take in-progress photos throughout printing and assembly. These count as evidence of your design process and make your portfolio much stronger. A photo of your first failed print is just as valuable as a photo of your finished model.
