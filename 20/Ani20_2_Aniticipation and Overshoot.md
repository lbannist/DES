# Animation 20 – Anticipation and Overshoot

## Animation Production Techniques: Selling Motion Through Preparation and Recovery

**Time:** 1 Week (4–5 Class Periods)

**Due Date:** Following Monday before class

---

# Learning Objectives

By the end of this assignment students will:

* Create convincing anticipation poses
* Demonstrate overshoot and settling motion
* Apply principles of weight and momentum
* Integrate secondary motion into an action sequence
* Improve motion readability through staging

---

# Production Question

## Why do professional animations feel more believable than real-life video?

The answer is often found in what happens before and after an action.

Strong animators emphasize:

* Preparation
* Action
* Recovery

These stages help the audience understand motion and feel the weight behind it.

---

# Part 1 – Motion Analysis (Day 1)

Study examples of:

* Jumping
* Throwing
* Landing
* Swinging
* Pushing

### Observation Questions

1. What movement happens before the action?
2. How does the body prepare?
3. Does the character stop immediately?
4. What parts continue moving after the action?
5. How does the action communicate weight?

---

# Part 2 – Planning the Action (Day 1)

Choose one action:

* Jump
* Throw
* Kick
* Sword swing
* Heavy object lift
* Original approved action

### Planning Sketches

Create:

* Anticipation Pose
* Action Pose
* Overshoot Pose
* Final Settle Pose

---

# Part 3 – Animation Production (Days 2–4)

### Document Settings

* 1920 × 1080
* 12 FPS

### Requirements

* 4–6 second animation
* Clear anticipation
* Main action
* Visible overshoot
* Settle motion
* At least one secondary motion element

Examples:

* Hair
* Clothing
* Cape
* Antenna
* Tail
* Loose accessory

---

# Part 4 – Efficiency Reflection (Day 5)

Answer:

1. Which pose was most important?
2. Which section required the most frames?
3. How did anticipation improve readability?
4. How did overshoot improve realism?
5. What would you simplify next time?

Professional animators focus on transitions—not just movement.
